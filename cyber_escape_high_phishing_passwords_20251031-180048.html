Cybersecurity Escape Room

High School - Phishing & Password Defense

You’ve infiltrated the digital fortress of a mysterious hacker group. To escape their trap, you must bypass five security “locks” placed in their phishing command center. Each lock tests your mastery of phishing recognition and password safety. Solve them all to escape before time runs out!

Lock 1: The Suspicious Email

You find an email from your “school IT admin” asking you to confirm your password by clicking a link. What’s the best way to handle this?

A) Click the link and enter your password to verify it.
B) Delete the email immediately without reading it.
C) Examine the sender’s address and report to IT if suspicious.
D) Forward the message to your friend to check their opinion.
Look carefully at sender addresses and links; authentic admins never ask for passwords via email.

Lock 2: The Link Trap

When analyzing a possible phishing link, which domain name detail should alert you first?

A) The link uses HTTPS encryption.
B) The domain name is similar but slightly misspelled.
C) The link includes your actual name.
D) It contains numbers and letters.
Attackers often use typos or subdomains like “micr0soft.com” to trick users.

Lock 3: Password Generator Vault

You must create a strong passphrase for a secure door. Which of the following best meets high-security standards?

A) 12345678
B) password123
C) $tarlit!C0mp@ss_27
D) mydogrex
Long, complex, unique passwords with mixed characters are best. Avoid common words or sequences.

Lock 4: The Multi-Factor Vault

Your next digital door requires multifactor authentication (MFA). Why is MFA critical against phishing attacks?

A) It makes logging in slower, annoying attackers.
B) It ensures that even if a password is stolen, another factor blocks access.
C) It automatically filters phishing emails.
D) It prevents all malware infection.
Think of MFA as an extra door—stealing your key (password) isn’t enough without another proof like your phone code.

Lock 5: Decoding a Phishing Example

The hackers left a “sample” email for new recruits:

Subject: Urgent Account Suspension! Click bank-verify-login.net to restore access.

Analyze the message. What specific feature most clearly identifies this as phishing?

A) Urgent tone and fake domain unrelated to the bank
B) Proper grammar and official logo
C) It’s sent from a trusted company
D) It asks for you to ignore the message
Scammers often create urgency and use deceptive domains to harvest credentials.
